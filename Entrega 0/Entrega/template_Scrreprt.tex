	\begin{abstract}
		This report analyzes a manufacturing scenario with three machines producing items at different defect rates for the first 3 parts of it...A,B,C. Exact Bayesian calculations are performed to obtain posterior probabilities of machine origin given defective or non-defective items. THen, a Monte Carlo simulation with \(N=100{,}000\) samples validates the theoretical results. We examine the Convergence of the simulation as a function of sample size. 
		
		Finally, the analysis is extended to a Poisson model for flaw counts in this case: For two machines, and the posterior probability of machine origin given a specific number of flaws is computed both analytically and via simulation.
	\end{abstract}
	
	\section{Initial conditions}
	A factory uses three machines (A, B, C) with production shares of 20\%, 50\%, and 30\%, respectively. Their defect rates are 10\%, 2\%, and 5\%.:
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item Exact Bayesian calculation of the probability that a defective (or non-defective) item came from each machine.
		\item Monte Carlo simulation to estimate these probabilities.
		\item Analysis of convergence of the Monte Carlo estimate with increasing sample size.
	\end{enumerate}
	All computations were implemented in Python using NumPy, Matplotlib.
	
	\section{Part A: Exact Bayesian Calculation}
	\subsection{Total Probability of Defect}
	Let \(P(A)=0.20\), \(P(B)=0.50\), \(P(C)=0.30\) be the prior probabilities. The defect rates are:
	\[
	P(\text{defect}|A)=0.10,\quad P(\text{defect}|B)=0.02,\quad P(\text{defect}|C)=0.05.
	\]
	By the law of total probability,
	\begin{align*}
		P(\text{defect}) &= P(\text{defect}|A)P(A) + P(\text{defect}|B)P(B) + P(\text{defect}|C)P(C) \\
		&= 0.10\times0.20 + 0.02\times0.50 + 0.05\times0.30 = 0.045.
	\end{align*}
	
	\subsection{Posterior Probabilities Given Defect (Bayes)}
	Applying Bayes' theorem:
	\[
	P(A|\text{defect}) = \frac{P(\text{defect}|A)P(A)}{P(\text{defect})} = \frac{0.10\times0.20}{0.045} \approx 0.4444.
	\]
	Similarly,
	\[
	P(B|\text{defect}) = \frac{0.02\times0.50}{0.045} \approx 0.2222,\qquad
	P(C|\text{defect}) = \frac{0.05\times0.30}{0.045} \approx 0.3333.
	\]
	These have to sum to 1.
	
	\subsection{Posterior Probabilities Given Non‑Defect}
	The probability of a non‑defective item is \(P(\text{non‑defect})=1-0.045=0.955\). The conditional probabilities of non‑defect are \(0.90\), \(0.98\), and \(0.95\) for A, B, C, respectively. Then:
	\begin{align*}
		P(A|\text{non‑defect}) &= \frac{0.90\times0.20}{0.955} \approx 0.1885,\\
		P(B|\text{non‑defect}) &= \frac{0.98\times0.50}{0.955} \approx 0.5131,\\
		P(C|\text{non‑defect}) &= \frac{0.95\times0.30}{0.955} \approx 0.2984.
	\end{align*}
	All these values are extracted from the code.
	
	\subsection{Interpretation}
	Machine B produces half of all items but has the lowest defect rate (2\%). Consequently, when a defective item is observed, it is less likely to have come from B (\(P(B|\text{defect})\approx 22.2\%\)) than from A (\(44.4\%\)) or C (\(33.3\%\)).
	
	\section{Part B: Monte Carlo Simulation}

Each item was assigned a machine according to the prior probabilities, and then its defect status was drawn from the corresponding defect rate. The fractions of defective and non‑defective items originating from each machine were computed.

	\section{Part D: Extension to the Poisson Distribution}
	Now suppose two machines (A and B) produce items with Poisson‑distributed flaw counts. Machine A accounts for 40\% of production with mean flaws \(\mu_A=3\); machine B accounts for 60\% with \(\mu_B=1\). The probability mass function is
	\[
	P(k|\mu) = \frac{\mu^k e^{-\mu}}{k!}.
	\]
	
	\subsection{Bayesian Posterior for Flaw Count \(k\)}
	Given an item with \(k\) flaws, the posterior probability that it came from machine A is
	\[
	P(A|k) = \frac{P(k|A)P(A)}{P(k|A)P(A) + P(k|B)P(B)}.
	\]
	Figure~\ref{fig:poisson} shows \(P(A|k)\) and \(P(B|k)\) for \(k=0,\dots,5\). As \(k\) increases, the evidence strongly favors machine A because of its higher mean flaw rate.
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{poisson.png}
		\caption{Posterior probability of machine origin given \(k\) flaws.}
		\label{fig:poisson}
	\end{figure}
	
	\subsection{Monte Carlo Verification for \(k=2\)}
	A simulation with \(N=200{,}000\) items was run.(Using the scipy library for the Poisson distribution) The analytical value for \(k=2\) is
	\[
	P(A|k=2) = \frac{\frac{3^2 e^{-3}}{2!}\cdot 0.4}{\frac{3^2 e^{-3}}{2!}\cdot 0.4 + \frac{1^2 e^{-1}}{2!}\cdot 0.6} \approx 0.4481.
	\]
	The simulation yielded \(0.449\) with an absolute error of \(0.001\), confirming the analytical result.

Monte Carlo simulation accurately reproduces these probabilities and demonstrates convergence with increasing sample size. The Poisson extension illustrates how additional information (flaw counts) can dramatically update posterior beliefs. All code and figures are available for reproducibility ignore the seed.
